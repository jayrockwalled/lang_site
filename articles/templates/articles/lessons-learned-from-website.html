Lessons Learned from Building this Website

How I Benefitted from Struggling / Self Learning

I originally started learning about web development in August, 2018. I already liked the idea of having my own website to show off various projects as well as having an outlet for my self-expression. All CS students from my undergrad has their own personal websites that they'd put on their resume so I assumed it would be pretty easy. Despite starting with almost no knowledge of web development, I thought I'd be able to grind through it in a week or two.

It was actually far from easy and ended up taking much more time than I thought it would. Counting time spent learning about new tools, building the website, troubleshooting, and figuring out hosting, I'd say this took easily more than 200 hours. During those 200 hours, I learned a lot about doing development as well as a lot about myself.

1. Google is my best friend

Using the correct keywords, the solution to almost all of my issues were a single Google search away. What I would have thought would take hours reading through documentation never lasted more than an hour.

2. Anaysis paralysis is Real

For any given problem or project, there're likely a plethora of solutions or means. Finding the most optimal path takes a lot of research. There were times I spent many hours looking into different tools and libraries, racking my brain to figure out the absolute best way of getting the task done. When I was trying to find the best way to host this site, I looked into AWS, Azure, Digital Ocean, and other hosting services. I got so deep in the research that I was constantly doubting myself and hesitating before getting to the next step. It took me a while to realize that the analysis was becoming counter productive and it would be best to just pick whichever is convenient and works best for me.

4. Learning is satisfying again

In college, I always wanted to finish my assignments as quickly as I possibly can. Any extra time spent struggling on homework problems meant less time participating in my hobbies or hanging out with friends. I absolutely hated to struggle on a problem longer than I deemed was necessary.

Working through this, I did not see it as a struggle. It felt more like one big learning experience in which I was felt engaged. I did not see troubleshooting through code or deciphering error messages as an obstruction like I used to. I saw it as all a part of the process to become a more skilled programmer.
